DRAWING NO. 002 — COGS 127 — REV. 2026.05

Coordinating the volunteers who run
church livestreams.

An 8-week team project redesigning Asana’s Sunday-service workflow for small-church tech volunteers.

STATUS · USER TESTING COURSE · COGS 127 · SP'26 INSTRUCTOR · PHILIP GUO TEAM · 4
Going in, we were pretty sure we knew what these teams needed, so we built four high-fidelity concepts to prove it. Then we put them in front of two people who actually run tech at a church. Turns out the features we were proudest of were the ones they cared about least — and that one round of testing is what steered the whole project.
§ 01 · THE PROBLEM

The people we designed for, and what actually breaks

At a small church, the whole livestream usually runs on a handful of volunteers. They set up the cameras, run the audio, control the slides, and keep the stream going every week — usually unpaid, and with a different mix of people showing up Sunday to Sunday.

Our two early interviews pointed us somewhere we didn't expect. Yeah, the audio and video break sometimes, but that's not really the issue. The actual problem is coordination. Roles change every week, people don't always show up, and most of the knowledge about how everything works lives inside one person's head. The week that person isn't there, Sunday is basically at risk. So what we set out to support was keeping the service running no matter who shows up, and that's why we ended up focusing on Asana's “Sunday Service” workspace instead of the streaming software itself.

§ 02 · WHAT WE TESTED

Four competing directions

Instead of polishing one idea to death, we built four high-fidelity screens. Each one was basically a bet on a different answer to the same question: what does this team actually need?

§ 03 · METHOD

Two users, one call each

We tested with two people who actually do this: one who leads the tech team across a few Sunday services, and one who runs the livestream himself. Both sessions were over Zoom, one person at a time. They clicked through the screens and just talked out loud about what they were seeing.

One thing I should be upfront about

These were walkthroughs of static screens, not hands-on usability tests, so I'm not going to pretend I have task times or error rates. We showed each screen and talked it through, and the useful part was really their gut reactions and what they said they'd actually use.

§ 04 · FINDINGS

What testing told us

A few reactions came up with both people and were strong enough that they ended up changing the whole direction of the project.

[ 01 ]

A plain list of volunteers wasn't really the problem.

At a small church, the leader already knows who their volunteers are. What they're actually stressing about before Sunday is whether everyone's confirmed and whether any role is still empty. So a screen that just lists people doesn't help them much on its own.

→ So we dropped the volunteer-first view as our main idea
[ 02 ]

The role-based view matched the question they actually ask every week.

Organizing by role and making the empty roles obvious lined up with the real question they have: do we have the right people for this service? Both of them did say that breaking everything down into tiny tasks was overkill, since volunteers already know what their job is.

→ Role-based planning became our main direction
[ 03 ]

Our two users split on skill-matching, so we dropped it.

One of them thought a skill-match label was useful for slotting the right person into a role. The other one thought it was just clutter. Instead of picking a side, we swapped it for a short guide (basically an SOP) for each role, so the role is easy to learn instead of something the app has to track.

→ Replaced skill-match with a short guide for each role
[ 04 ]

Nobody cares about a "who's online" view once the service starts.

We'd put a ton of work into showing who was covering each role during the service. Both users told us that part wasn't useful — by the time service starts, the team is all together in the back and already knows who showed up. What the live screen should really do is help them catch and fix the stuff that breaks.

→ Refocused the live mode on problems, not who's around
[ 05 ]

The livestream itself is the real blind spot.

During the service, the operator isn't actually watching the YouTube or Facebook feed. The way he finds out it dropped is when someone texts him. The one thing he got really excited about was a feature that catches when the stream cuts out and pings him.

→ Added livestream monitoring and alerts
§ 05 · BEFORE & AFTER

Three things that change for the person running Sunday

Each of these starts with something a user told us they deal with right now, paired with the design change it led to.

STORY 01"Do we have everyone for Sunday?"
[ BEFORE ]

To figure out who's missing, the tech lead has to swipe through each service time in Planning Center one by one and hold it all in his head. Gaps show up late, sometimes not until it's already a problem.

[ AFTER ]

A readiness dashboard with a tab for each service time. Any unfilled role shows up in red the second he opens it, and he can check availability in one tap. Now coverage is the first thing he sees instead of the last thing he finds out.

STORY 02"The stream went down and we didn't know"
[ BEFORE ]

During the service, the operator is locked in on the sound in the room, not the actual broadcast. If the stream drops, the first he hears about it is a viewer texting him — usually after minutes of dead air.

[ AFTER ]

The live dashboard watches the stream and fires an alert the moment something breaks, so the team can jump on it right away instead of finding out after the fact.

STORY 03"The same few problems, every week"

Most of the problems we solve are the same few things over and over again. — TECH LEAD, USER INTERVIEW

[ BEFORE ]

Problems get fixed in the moment and then forgotten. Nothing gets written down, so the team keeps hitting and re-solving the same handful of issues week after week.

[ AFTER ]

Now issues get logged during the service and can be turned into follow-up tasks. Afterward the team can look back, spot the pattern, and update the role's guide so the same thing doesn't keep coming back.

§ 06 · DIRECTION

From four ideas down to one

Testing basically collapsed the four directions into one clear point of view. The product should help a team answer two questions: do we have the right people confirmed for each service, and can we see and respond fast when something goes wrong? What it shouldn't become is a heavier task tracker or a big volunteer database nobody asked for.

[ PART 01 ]
Sunday Readiness Dashboard
A manager view organized by service time: roles, who's assigned, who's confirmed, and what's still missing.
[ PART 02 ]
Volunteer Role View
A simple screen that shows a volunteer their service time, their role, who they're working with, and the guide for that role.
[ PART 03 ]
Live Issue Diagnosis
A live view that flags problems, suggests a fix, logs them, and can spin them into tasks.
